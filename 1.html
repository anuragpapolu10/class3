Giant Rock In The Sea Is Very Beautiful

by Anurag Papolu

Differential erosion is a simple concept with a beautiful impact. Variability in rock hardness changes how it is sculpted by water and wind. It's a bit of a tease that geoscientists look at any strange landscape and attribute it to differential erosion, but it's not far off.

Rock hardness depends on the chemical composition and formation conditions of a rock impacts its strength. The farther a rock is from its formation conditions, the less at equilibrium it is with its surrounding environment. Minerals that form under surface conditions of temperature, pressure, and moisture are most resistant to weathering at the surface, while minerals that form deep within the Earth are weakest at the surface. Rocks that are crystalline solids are more resistant to weathering, while those with cracks, joints, and fractures have more exposed surface area prone to weathering. When rocks with different weathering properties are adjacent to each other, the result can be beautiful.
